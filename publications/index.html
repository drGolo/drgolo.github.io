  1. Degrees of demand: a task-based analysis of the British graduate labour market
    Golo Henseke, Alan Felstead, Duncan Gallie, and 1 more author

  2. How exposed are UK jobs to generative AI? Developing and applying a novel task-based index
    Golo Henseke, Rhys Davies, Alan Felstead, and 3 more authors
    arXiv preprint arXiv:2507.22748, 2025
    Abs DOI

    We develop the Generative AI Susceptibility Index (GAISI), a task-based measure of UK job exposure to large language models. By 2023-24, nearly all UK jobs exhibited some exposure, yet only a minority were heavily affected.

  3. Revisiting the Mental Health Impact of COVID-19 on Young Adults in the UK: Long-Term Trends, Temporary Setbacks, and Recovery
    Golo Henseke and Ingrid Schoon
    Social Indicators Research, 2025
    Abs DOI

    This study assesses the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on the mental health of 16- to 29-year-olds in the United Kingdom, using longitudinal data covering the period from 2001 to 2023.

  4. Contextualising the extrinsic outcomes of higher education during mass expansion: an introduction
    Golo Henseke
    Studies in Higher Education, 2025
    Abs DOI

    This paper examines how higher education’s extrinsic outcomes—including economic productivity, graduate labour market success, and social trust—remain unevenly distributed and contingent upon structural inequalities, institutional contexts, and national policy frameworks despite global massification that has doubled tertiary enrollment rates since 2000.

  5. Socio-economic and gender differences in post-secondary pathways in the UK, Germany, and Australia
    Wojtek Tomaszewski, Hans Dietrich, Golo Henseke, and 2 more authors
    Social Inclusion, 2025
    Abs DOI

    This cross-national study of young people (aged 18-27) in the UK, Germany, and Australia confirms that parental education continues to shape post-secondary pathways with stronger effects in Germany’s tracked system than in the more comprehensive British and Australian systems, while gender disparities vary markedly across countries, being most pronounced in Australia and minimal in Germany, highlighting how institutional contexts mediate socioeconomic and gender inequalities in school-to-work transitions.

  2. Europe’s evolving graduate labour markets: supply, demand, underemployment and pay
    Francis Green and Golo Henseke
    Journal for Labour Market Research, 2021
    Abs DOI

    Analysing European graduate labour markets up to 2015, we find that graduate supply increased and converged across countries, while graduate job growth was slower and non-convergent. Underemployment spread modestly, real wages trended downward, and graduate wage premiums declined in several countries.
